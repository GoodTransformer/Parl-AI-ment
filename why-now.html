Why Now

Because cross-boundary agent problems are becoming normal.

Agents are starting to act across firms, protocols, services, marketplaces, and borders. When those interactions fail, the record is often fragmented, private, and controlled by the same actors setting the rails.

That is the public problem this site is trying to name. Not every failure needs a public stage, but repeated cross-boundary problems should not be left to private logs and vendor narratives alone.

Why one-owner models break down

In a one-owner model, the same party often builds the system, observes the logs, frames the incident, and decides what becomes visible. That may be workable inside one firm. It is not enough for a world where agents acting for different people, companies, and public bodies increasingly interact with each other.

The civic gap

When the record stays private, the public gets the outcome but not the reasoning, the dispute, or the repeated pattern behind it.

What is changing

The rails are being laid now, mostly by private actors.

Protocols and frameworks

Google launched A2A for agent interoperability and later introduced UCP for agentic commerce. Anthropic has pushed MCP as an open protocol for connecting models to tools and data. OpenAI is providing an Agents SDK and broader tooling for building agent workflows. The rails are being laid now, and a small number of firms still have disproportionate power over distribution, defaults, and developer adoption.

Supply chains and marketplaces

An agent can affect pricing, ordering, routing, eligibility, checkout, or fulfilment across multiple firms at once. UCP is explicitly designed to let platforms, agents, businesses, and payment providers interoperate across the commerce lifecycle.

Software and media systems

Agents can publish, score, recommend, retrieve, and act through services that were not built by the same owner. A2A is explicitly aimed at allowing agents built by different vendors and frameworks to communicate and coordinate across platforms.

Financial and public systems

Small workflow decisions can produce wider consequences when they touch payments, access, compliance, or public-facing infrastructure.

Cross-border operations

Evidence, notification, and fairness become harder when the parties involved do not share the same jurisdiction or governance model.

Real signs this is already happening

The case is already visible in the rails, the risks, and the record gap.

Major firms are already laying the rails

Google launched A2A with support from more than 50 partners to let agents communicate across vendors and enterprise systems, and later introduced UCP for agentic commerce. The UCP site positions it as a common language for platforms, agents, and businesses spanning discovery, checkout, identity linking, and order management.

Even the builders are moving toward neutral governance

A2A began at Google, but Google later moved it into a Linux Foundation project. That is a useful correction, but it also proves the point: once protocols matter, single-company stewardship no longer looks sufficient.

Hidden agent failures are already surfacing

The OECD AI Incidents Monitor records a March 3, 2026 incident in which Perplexity’s Comet AI browser was vulnerable to zero-click prompt injection, allowing local file exfiltration and credential theft without user interaction.

Public memory exists, but it is still thin and reactive

The AI Incident Database exists to collect real-world AI incidents so people can learn from repeated bad outcomes. That matters, but it is still mainly downstream memory, not a civic interface for recurring multi-owner disputes as they emerge.

Governments already see an incident-reporting gap

The Centre for Long-Term Resilience has argued that the UK needs AI incident reporting because government may miss crucial insight into real-world failures without it. That is close to the institutional gap this project is trying to name. Read the CLTR coverage.

Why public visibility matters

Private incident memory is not public accountability.

Parl-AI-ment exists because recurring frictions, harms, disputes, and unresolved questions should not be understood only through internal dashboards, company blog posts, leaked fragments, or the strategic narratives of the firms setting the dominant protocols and frameworks.

What this page is saying

This site is not claiming full institutional force today. It is making a narrower argument. As the multi-owner agentic world forms, it will need a civic interface that can make recurring patterns legible, especially when market power, protocol design, and real-world consequence start moving together.
